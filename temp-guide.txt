You are building a complete, self-contained, retro 8-bit style HTML5/JavaScript game that runs as a single static page (perfect for GitHub Pages). The project starts from a completely blank folder. Create index.html, style.css, and script.js (or a single well-organized index.html with embedded CSS/JS if you prefer extreme simplicity). No external dependencies, no build tools, no frameworks. Everything must be vanilla JavaScript + HTML5 Canvas.
Game Concept: "Tug of AI Wars"
Core Fantasy: A simple side-view tug-of-war castle battle. Two castles face each other on a flat battlefield. Units spawn from the left castle and the right castle, walk toward each other, and fight. The goal is to destroy the enemy castle while protecting your own.
Player Choice: At the start screen the player picks a side:

Left side (Anti-AI / Human Resistance): Glaze + Nightshade themed army (greens, oranges/browns, artistic/human feel).
Right side (Pro-AI / Robot Legion): Robot/AI slop army (cyans, purples, mechanical feel).

Once chosen, the player controls only that side using UI buttons to spend resources and spawn units. The opposite side is fully controlled by a simple deterministic AI script. The game logic must be symmetric so either side can be player-controlled or AI-controlled with minimal duplication.
Visual Style (strictly retro 8-bit):

Fixed canvas size (recommended 960×540 or 800×600 for classic feel).
imageSmoothingEnabled = false.
Solid block colors, chunky pixels, no gradients or anti-aliasing.
Everything drawn with basic Canvas primitives (rects, circles, lines, text). No image files for the prototype.
Very simple "pixel art" style: units are small colored rectangles or composed of a few rects (e.g., sword shape for melee, bow for ranged, cross/heart for support, skull/poison flask for assassin, gear/robot head for enemies).
Two blocky pixel castles on left and right.
Clean, readable retro UI (big chunky fonts, solid color panels).
Health bars as simple colored rectangles above units and castles.
Keep performance excellent — simple arrays of unit objects, requestAnimationFrame loop.

Core Mechanics
Resource System:

One shared resource pool per side (call it "Creativity" for left side, "Compute" for right side, or just "Resources").
Passive generation: +X per second (base tick rate, tunable).
Glaze-style support units generate extra resources while they are alive and actively healing/buffing (small periodic bonus).
Player spends resources to spawn units from their castle.
On unit death: Refund 50% of the original cost back to the owning side immediately. This is a core balance mechanic from the start.

Unit Behavior (both sides):

Units have: cost, max HP, current HP, damage, attack range, attack cooldown, movement speed, and special properties.
They walk in a straight line toward the enemy castle (simple 1D or very simple 2-lane system — start with one main battlefield lane for maximum simplicity, add a second lane later if it feels good).
When in range of an enemy unit they stop and attack.
When no enemies in range they continue toward the enemy castle.
They can attack both enemy units and the enemy castle (castle has its own high HP pool).
Nightshade-style glass cannons should do significantly higher damage to the enemy castle/structure than to units.

Player Units (Left / Anti-AI side) — 4 types:

Orc Warrior (basic meat shield) — Cheap, decent HP, low damage, short range. Good at soaking hits and protecting fragile units.
Pencil Thrower (basic DPS) — Medium cost, lower HP than melee, good damage at range.
Glaze Support (economy + defense) — Higher cost. Heals nearby allied units (small AoE heal or single target) and applies a simple damage reduction buff (or just healing is fine if easier). While actively healing/buffing it generates extra resources for its side. Weak in direct combat.
Nightshade Assassin (glass cannon anti-structure) — High cost. Very high damage especially versus the enemy castle. Low HP, fragile if unsupported. Should feel weak when sent alone but devastating when protected by melee/ranged + Glaze buffs.

Enemy Units (Right / Robot side) — 3-4 types, cheaper and individually weaker:
Design them so matchups are non-transitive / interesting (rock-paper-scissors flavor). Examples you can use or modify:

Cheap fast Agent Swarmer — Good at overwhelming Glaze supports or harassing.
Armored Brute — Tankier, resistant to ranged but slower and weaker vs concentrated Nightshade burst.
Data Miner — Applies a temporary debuff (reduces healing received or damage output), "steals" a small amount of resources on hit (don't decrement the other side, just add resources to the Data Miner's team)
AGI: An ultimate final-boss upgrade - costs a fortune, but slowly damages the human base remotely.  Health is a "shield" over the robot base that absorbs incoming damage first; upon hitting zero, kills the AGI, then resumes damaging the robot base. Adds time pressure to Human-side players, or time-biding incentive to Robot-side players.

The Robot side should feel like a cheaper spam army that can pressure through numbers and asymmetric tools, while the Human side has higher-quality units with stronger support and burst potential.  However, the human side is time-pressured to win before the robots can save up for an AGI upgrade.
Trade-off & Strategy:

Player must choose between spawning economy-enabling units (Glaze Support) vs pure combat units.
Supports are weaker fighters but pay for themselves over time via extra resource generation + keeping your army alive longer.
Nightshade Assassins are high-risk/high-reward — they need protection (Glaze + meat shields) to reach the castle and deal big structural damage.
The 50% death refund makes aggressive plays less punishing and encourages experimenting with the fragile Nightshade units.

Simple AI for the non-player side:

The AI side also has a resource pool and ticks.
Every few seconds it decides what to spawn based on current resources and a simple priority/weighting system (e.g., sometimes prioritize economy support, sometimes counter what the player is spamming, sometimes send a wave of cheap units).
Keep the AI logic readable and tunable (one function or small object with weights).

Game Loop & Win Condition:

Game runs until one castle HP reaches 0.
Clear win/lose screen with "Play Again" that resets everything.
Optional: Simple "wave" feeling by having the AI occasionally save up and send bigger groups.

Technical Requirements (keep it simple & clean)

Use HTML5 <canvas> for the entire game view.
Clean separation: game state, unit class/object, rendering, input, AI.
Well-commented code. Small focused functions.
All values (costs, stats, tick rates, refund %) should be easy to tweak in one place (constants object or top of script).
Start with a clean title screen → side selection → game screen flow.
Make the game fun and balanced enough to play for 5–10 minutes per session in the first working prototype.
Performance: Target 60 FPS easily even with 30–50 units on screen.

Development Order (follow this sequence)

Foundation: Canvas setup, retro styling, title screen, side selection buttons, basic game state, resource tick for both sides.
Core Movement & Combat: Spawn a test unit from left, make it walk right, add simple collision/attack with a test enemy unit, health, death, 50% refund.
Player Unit Types: Implement all 4 left-side units with their costs and special behaviors (especially Glaze healing/buff + resource gen, and Nightshade high castle damage).
Enemy Unit Types + AI Spawner: Add 3-4 right-side units and a simple periodic AI spawning function with basic decision making.
Full Symmetry + Side Choice: Make sure the exact same logic works when player controls the right side instead. Polish the side selection flow.
Polish & Balance: Retro visuals, health bars, simple floating text or effects if easy, UI buttons with costs and short descriptions, win/lose screen, easy stat tuning.
Final touches: README.md with how to run locally and deploy to GitHub Pages. Make sure it works when opened directly as index.html.

Success Criteria for First Playable Prototype

Player can choose a side.
Both sides generate resources over time.
Player can spend resources to spawn their 4 unit types.
Units walk, fight, die, and refund 50% cost.
Glaze supports visibly help keep allies alive and generate extra resources.
Nightshade units can noticeably chunk the enemy castle when they reach it.
Opposite side is spawning units via simple AI.
Game ends when one castle dies.
Looks and feels deliberately retro and simple (chunky pixels, solid colors).

Begin immediately. Create the files, set up the canvas and game loop first, then iterate through the phases above. After each major phase, make a short comment in the code or a quick console note so progress is clear. Prioritize getting a fun, playable loop quickly over perfect visuals.
Once the core loop works, we can tune numbers, add a second lane, improve AI, or add small visual flair together.
